My Grand Stand

Submitted by KyleeP on Fri, 2011-08-26 17:13

Last November, a friend of mine, who had never met a bacon cheeseburger or slice of pepperoni pizza he didn’t like, bought me Jonathan Safran Foer’s Eating Animals. It was a just-for-the-hell-of-it gift, and one he soon regretted giving. Thanks to that book--and his thoughtfulness--I became a vegetarian, forever to be a disoriented wanderer through the meat-packed landscape of American cuisine.

“How will you get protein?”

“What’s going to be the main thing on your plate since vegetables are just sides?”

“Some vegetarians still eat seafood. Are you going to be one of them?”

These are just some of the many questions friends and family asked me when I first told them I had decided to give up meat. The funny thing? I couldn’t answer any of these questions because, to be honest, I hadn’t actually thought it through. What would I eat?!

The last nine months have been an adventure to say the least, and certainly not an easy one. I have committed myself to a lifestyle that makes me an anomaly in a country where many towns have more McDonald’s restaurants than they do post offices and where meat is commonly served at every meal. While it was an easy decision to make at the time of reading Foer’s account of his visits large factory farms that are the pillar of America’s agricultural industry, it has not been an easy one to stick to. I have been tempted numerous times by burgers and pulled pork sandwiches and, while I hate to admit it, have even had a few slip-ups.

But what does this have to do with living Completely Eco?

At Thanksgiving dinner, my first meal with my entire family after making the decision to completely alter my diet, my brother asked why I wasn’t eating turkey since I had at every meal in previous years. I explained that after reading Eating Animals I was appalled at the havoc factory farming practices are wreaking on the environment and our health and that I no longer wanted to support such an industry. He responded with a laugh: “You’re just one person. You giving up meat is not going to bring down an entire industry, so what’s the point?”

I understand what he’s saying, and I agree; I’m not going to bring down an entire industry on my own. But I’m sure as hell going to do what I can. And that, to me, is the point. The point is to do my own part to live in a way that I believe is more eco-friendly and to share my story with others. I am not insisting that everyone be like me and become a vegetarian. If you enjoy meat then, by all means, continue enjoying it without guilt. Just be aware of what you’re eating and where it’s coming from. Buy organic and locally-sourced meat whenever possible so as to make sure the antibiotics and steroids in factory-farmed animals’ diets don’t make their way into your own. Plus, who doesn’t love to support local farmers?

As the saying goes, life is all about choices. And I have made the choice to do my part for the environment and my health, even at the cost of limiting myself. Just like Jonathan Safran Foer, I might be an animal who eats, but I am no longer eating animals.
