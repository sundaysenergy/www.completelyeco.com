Don’t Have a Blue Christmas, Have a Green One!

With the myriad of things you have to do this Christmas: decorating, buying presents, baking cookies, planning holiday parties, wrapping gifts, traveling to Grandmas, and fitting in religious observance; there leaves very little room to consider the holiday season’s impact on the environment. But maybe try these sobering facts on for size:

• Wrapping paper and shopping bags account for 4 million tons of trash annually
• A natural tree will generate 3.1 kg of greenhouse gasses each year, and an artificial tree will generate 8.1 kg
• The 2.65 billion Christmas cards sold each year in the U.S. could fill a football field 10 stories high. If we each sent one card less, we'd save 50,000 cubic yards of paper
• From Thanksgiving to New Year’s Day, household waste increases by more than 25%. Added food waste, shopping bags, packaging, wrapping paper, bows and ribbons - it all adds up to an additional 1 million tons a week to our landfills
• if every family reused just two feet of holiday ribbon, the 38,000 miles of ribbon saved could tie a bow around the entire planet
• At least 28 billion pounds of edible food are wasted each year - or over 100 pounds per person
• Half of the paper America consumes is used to wrap and decorate consumer products
• Each year, 50 million Christmas trees are purchased in the U.S. Of those, about 30 million go to the landfill
• About 40% of all battery sales occur during the holiday season
• If each family reduced holiday gasoline consumption by one gallon (about twenty miles), we'd reduce greenhouse gas emissions by one million tons.
While these stats are staggering, and sobering, there are many actions (most of them very simple) that you can take to make the holidays less wasteful, and more joyful.

The Christmas Tree
Considered by many to be the quintessential symbol of the holidays, the Christmas tree is also a major cause of environmental concern. Depending on the study results you consult, there is a case for either artificial or natural being better (or worse) for the environment. No matter what route you take, there are ways to lessen the damage.
• If going the natural route, support small, organic Christmas tree farms in your area; or get a permit to cut a tree on National Forest land (thus helping with needed maintenance)
• Choose a living tree and plant it later.
• Buy and decorate a potted Norfolk pine, they will make your home lovely year round
• Many communities offer free pick up for Christmas tree recycling, where they turn it into mulch to be used around the community, such as parks. In Baltimore County the pick-up starts January 10th. Go to: http://www.baltimorecountymd.gov/News/holiday_winter/treecollection.html for more info.
• You can strip the needles from the branches to make potpourri, or to be used in cooking
• The needles can also be used to balance a compost heap- their acidity helps balance out alkaline components such as wood ash.
• The branches can be used to make Christmas decorations for the next year

Wrapping Paper, Cards, and Shopping Bags
Tis the season… to spend a lot of money. In fact, the average American will spend $750 on gifts for the holidays; and all those gifts need to be bagged, wrapped, tagged… Well you get the idea. Yet for all the waste that goes into gift giving, there are some practical remedies.
• Bring your own reusable shopping bags when you go shopping
• Use large paper bags from department stores to put used wrapping paper in to be recycled
• Shred used wrapping paper to be used as fun, protective lining for future gifts
• Old wrapping paper is great for cleaning windows, and leaving them streak free
• Christmas cards with fun patters can be cut into gift tags
• Use recycled gift wrap
• Reuse gift bags you’ve received and re-gift them to others

Turkey, Stuffing, and Leftovers
There are only so many “leftovers sandwiches” you can eat before you give up and want to throw it all away, but here are some more fun options for the obvious leftovers, and some for the less obvious.
• www.foodnetwork.com offers a lot of recipes, from the easy to advanced, for your holiday leftovers
• Boil up bits of skin and bones with onion, garlic, herbs, and left over bits of raw veggies (such as celery and carrots) to make a stock to be used in soups and stews. Extra points for this one as the stock can be frozen and used later
• Turn your leftovers (except for meat, fish, and dairy) into compost to be used in flower beds… or to replant that live Christmas tree! (check with your local waste management authority for rules and regulations in your area)
So keep the Holidays a joyous time, not a wasteful time, by following some, or all, of these helpful tips! And make this winter season, Green!
